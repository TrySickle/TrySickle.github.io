Summary

The guiding motivation behind this project came from Carl
DiSalvo’s ideas of adversarial design and articulation which he uses to critique
ubicomp devices. DiSalvo describes adversarial design as “designs [that] do the
work of agonism,” that is, provokes political conflict for the purpose of
creating productive debate. His concept of devices of articulation has to do
with the connections ubicomp devices create that “extends outward to enroll
people, other entities in the environment, and even the environment itself.”

This exhibit is itself a digital media artifact and in
focusing on nature as a political force, it connects media artifacts together
in order to highlight certain aspects of their messages. I chose two artifacts
from each of the Pinterest collections that I could find political meaning within,
in order to achieve a balance of representation across media forms. The overall
message should be that politics pervades most media and that not all
connections are immediately obvious. After experiencing the exhibit, the effect
should be an appreciation for media creators and a greater understanding of how
media can vary but share certain themes.

Douglas Kellner’s ideas of critical media analysis also play
a role in this digital exhibit. Kellner promotes a threefold approach of production
and political economy, textual analysis, and audience reception in order to
truly consume media and understand it well. The exhibit should promote critical
thinking by helping the audience find connections it may have missed and
therefore providing a platform for readers to begin thinking of their own
connections. Media becomes a very political subject under Kellner’s analysis as
a way that content creators and larger organizations can inject their thoughts
into the minds of any unsuspecting consumers. The goal of this project is not quite
as dramatic as Kellner’s ideas on cultural analysis, but the idea of greater
media literacy is important in creating a modern populace that is ready to enter
the endless media of the internet. By taking a detailed look at different forms
of media, the exhibit pushes the audience to form its own opinions and perhaps
carry this attitude to the greater world of media beyond this site.

Another important idea that motivated this project was Lev
Manovich’s theory of the database as media. Manovich distinguishes a database
as “a list of items which it refuses to order” versus a narrative which
“creates a cause-and-effect trajectory of seemingly unordered items.” This
digital collection of artifacts lies somewhere in between a database and a
narrative since it establishes some organization of artifacts within the circle
and provides some cause-and-effect relationships between artifacts. As a
database, the individual artifacts of the collection can be experienced in any
order and no knowledge will be lost as a result. Using the affordances of the
digital media, the exhibit is more like an interactive narrative, or
“hyper-narrative” as Manovich calls it, in reference to hypertext links.
